I'm a PhD candidate under Jon Cagan and Phil Leduc in Mechanical Engineering at CMU, and part of the Stanford Crowd Research Collective.

I make computers design.

Quality creative thinking is highly prized because it is remarkably hard to achieve. As a consequence, companies face financial uncertainty and time pressure when trying to innovate. My work focuses on how we might make creative decisions in the future, designing faster and at higher quality than we do today. This research leverages design, society and artificial intelligence, with the following specific interests.

A consistent difficulty in automating design is having a reliable representation that can encompass the important nuances of the design problem.

In my PhD I have built an automatic, grammar like way of representing structured systems, such as designs. We applied this tool to design, architecture, abstract systems, and even biological domains. We also developed tools for detecting differences and anomalies between cases of this type of data, faster and more accurately than previous efforts.

This work is with Jon Cagan and Phil Leduc, and has been published in Design Computing and Cognition (DCC, 2016) and in a forthcoming issue of Artificial Intelligence for Engineering Design, Analysis and Manufacturing (AIEDAM). It is also currently under patent application.

Despite constant improvements in intelligent algorithms, we are still a surprisingly long way from having ones that can replace every aspect of human labor.

Mentoring several groups of undergraduate students at CMU and in the Pitt i3 program, we have worked to build systems that are made stronger by encouraging humans and computers to work together. We have focused particularly on how human-computer hybrid systems can have more insight about creative problems and products than just the human or just the computer. We have also explored ways that novice humans can be supported by computer systems to provide rich design insight.

This work has been presented at the iConference, and is under review for further publications.

Crowdsourcing tools today offer ad hoc and remote work arrangements at an unprecedented scale, yet many of the benefits of having a traditional job have not yet been realized for this community.

I am part of the Stanford Crowd Research Collective which is a group of over 1000 collaborators from all over the world, led by Stanford professor Michael Bernstein. We conduct research as a crowd, and are developing a new crowdsourcing platform that aims to mitigate many of the shortcomings of crowdsourcing today: Daemo, which was demonstrated at CSCW 2017.

We have studied how crowdsourcing systems could better support the communities they serve. We introduced crowd guilds at CSCW 2017, a way to improve reputation and feedback within crowdsourcing, inspired by traditional worker guilds, and have developed the Daemo Constitution, a mechanism for self governance in crowdsourcing marketplaces which will be presented at Collective Intelligence, 2017

People are complex and only partially rational. As a consequence, incentives in socio-technical systems' can drastically influence how effectively people use them. With algorithmic game theory, a branch of economics, incentives can be carefully designed to achieve specific behavioral goals. Many socio-technical systems today stand to benefit from incorporating these lessons.

Together with Dilrukshi Gamage, Thejan Rajapakshe, Haritha Thilakarathne, Indika Perera, and Shantha Fernando, we have explored how aligning the incentives for peer feedback in online learning helps improve the quality of the feedback. We will present this work at Learning at Scale, 2017.

A similar line of thinking and incentive design has been used in much of the work with the Stanford Crowd Research Collective in our crowd guilds and boomerang projects to improve interactions on crowdsourcing marketplaces.
